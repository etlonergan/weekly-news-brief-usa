The

Common Sense

Prompt

In 1776, Thomas Paine wrote Common Sense to cut through propaganda
and give ordinary Americans a shared foundation for judgment —
in plain language, free to all.

This prompt is built on the same conviction.

A free, open prompt for any AI assistant. Nine stories every week. Facts sourced only from wire services and official records. Every perspective names the outlets consulted — so you can verify and challenge what you read.

The antithesis of algorithmic news. Free to copy, share, and adapt without restriction.

"No AI model is perfectly neutral, and this prompt does not claim to be. What it claims is transparency. Every fact is sourced. Every perspective names where it came from. And if anything seems incomplete or wrong, you are always invited to say so."

          The Common Sense Prompt does not claim to be perfectly neutral —
          no source can make that claim honestly. Instead it earns trust
          through three structural transparency mechanisms the reader can
          verify for themselves.
        

        

        

          
The Reader Challenge Mechanism

          
After every perspectives section, the prompt adds a standing invitation:
             "If any perspective here seems incomplete or misrepresented, type 2 and tell me how it should read."

          
If a reader offers a correction, the model incorporates it transparently —
             noting that a correction was offered and what it was. The prompt is
             designed to be improved by its readers, not defended against them.

        

        

          
Symmetric Sourcing Requirement

          
For every domestic US story, the prompt requires a minimum of two
             conservative-leaning sources and two progressive-leaning sources to
             be consulted — regardless of which side is more prominent in the
             news cycle that week. Symmetry is structural, not editorial.
